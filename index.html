Learn to Drive Smart

Chapter 1

Chapter 1 of Learn to Drive Smart focuses on the driver’s role in making responsible choices to ensure safety on the road. It introduces the concept of being a “thinking driver” and outlines essential considerations for making good driving decisions, staying alert, and being responsible for others on the road.

Be a Thinking Driver

A thinking driver prioritizes safety by making careful decisions and staying aware of risks. Every action you take while driving—whether it’s choosing the safest route or reacting to changing traffic conditions—affects your own safety and that of others.

Key elements of being a thinking driver include:

  • Be fit to drive: Drivers must be mentally and physically alert. Fatigue, illness, anger, or distraction can impair judgment and reaction times. Avoid driving if you are overtired or emotionally distressed, and never drive under the influence of drugs or alcohol, as this is one of the leading causes of accidents.

  • Make good decisions: Good drivers make quick, accurate decisions. The chapter presents scenarios, like being tempted to run a yellow light when pressed for time or considering sending a text while driving, to emphasize the importance of resisting risky behaviors. A thinking driver plans ahead, predicts potential hazards, and focuses on the road at all times.

  • Take responsibility: Driving requires you to take responsibility not only for your own actions but also for the safety of your passengers and other road users. This means being aware of other drivers, pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists, and making decisions that prioritize everyone’s safety.

Be Fit to Drive

Being in the right condition to drive is essential:

  • Emotional state: Feeling angry, frustrated, or stressed can cloud your judgment and affect how you react to road situations. Calm yourself before driving.
  • Fatigue: Driving while tired reduces your ability to make quick decisions and increases the likelihood of falling asleep at the wheel. Even if you don’t fall asleep, tiredness can impair your focus and reaction time.
  • Impairment by substances: Driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs severely affects your ability to make decisions and react to changing road conditions. Impaired drivers are much more likely to cause serious accidents.

Make Good Decisions

Good driving is about constant decision-making, and it’s essential to make safe choices:

  • Learning continuously: Becoming a good driver doesn’t end with getting your licence. You’ll keep learning as you gain more driving experience. Over time, you’ll encounter new road conditions and changes in vehicle technology or driving laws, all of which require ongoing attention.
  • Planning your driving: Safe driving involves planning ahead, such as allowing enough time for trips, knowing your route, and anticipating the need to equip your vehicle for different driving conditions (e.g., winter driving).
  • Predicting the scene: Smart drivers scan their surroundings, pay attention to road signs and traffic signals, and anticipate what other drivers or pedestrians might do. For example, you should be prepared for a child to suddenly dart out into the street or for a car ahead of you to stop unexpectedly.

Think for Yourself

Drivers need to develop independent judgment, particularly when faced with external pressures:

  • Influence from other drivers: At times, other drivers may pressure you into making unsafe choices, such as tailgating, honking, or flashing lights to encourage speeding. A thinking driver resists these influences and makes decisions based on safety.
  • Media influence: Advertisements and movies often portray driving in a way that emphasizes speed or risky behavior. It’s important to recognize and resist these unrealistic influences, focusing instead on safe driving habits.
  • Peer influence: Friends and passengers can also pressure you into unsafe driving practices, such as speeding, racing, or turning up the music too loudly. As the driver, it’s your responsibility to ensure safety, even if it means resisting peer pressure.

Take Responsibility

As a licensed driver, you are responsible for the safety of yourself, your passengers, and others on the road:

  • You, the driver: Acknowledge your own limits as a driver and understand your vehicle’s capabilities. This includes recognizing when you might be too tired or unwell to drive and making sure your vehicle is in good condition.

  • Your passengers: Ensure that all passengers wear seatbelts and are properly secured, especially children who require specific car seats or booster seats depending on their age and size. The chapter stresses the importance of responsible behavior, like asking passengers to buckle up and ensuring no one is in danger due to reckless driving.

  • Other road users: Consider the safety of others, including pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists. For example, slowing down in areas where children might be playing or yielding to pedestrians at crosswalks.

Driving Scenarios

Throughout the chapter, scenarios are presented to help drivers reflect on how they would respond in real-life situations:

  • Time pressure and emotions: One scenario describes a driver running late for an appointment while dealing with anger after an argument. The scenario asks what choices you would make—focus on the argument, or concentrate on driving safely? It emphasizes the importance of staying focused and managing emotions.
  • Traffic signals: Another example discusses approaching a yellow traffic light. The driver is faced with the decision to stop or go through the light when pressed for time. The scenario illustrates the importance of making safe decisions, even when time pressures make it tempting to take risks.

Other Considerations

  • Smoking and passengers: It’s illegal to smoke in a vehicle with passengers under 16 years old. Children are particularly vulnerable to the harmful effects of second-hand smoke, which can have serious health consequences.

  • Seatbelt safety: The chapter emphasizes the importance of seatbelt use. Statistics show that one in four people killed in car crashes were not wearing a seatbelt. Drivers are responsible for ensuring all passengers are buckled up.

Chapter 1 of Learn to Drive Smart stresses that driving is not just about getting from one place to another—it’s about making smart, safe choices at every step of the journey. Drivers must be fit, make good decisions, and take responsibility for themselves and others on the road.

Chapter 2

Chapter 2 of Learn to Drive Smart focuses on understanding your vehicle and ensuring it is set up and maintained for safe driving. It covers everything from adjusting your seat and mirrors to performing essential pre-trip checks to keep your vehicle in good condition.

Adjust for Safety

Before driving, you need to ensure that your vehicle is adjusted to fit you properly. This section covers key adjustments:

  • Seat: Your seat should be upright, allowing you to comfortably reach the pedals and steering wheel while keeping a slight bend in your legs. Make sure you can press the brake pedal fully with your right foot without stretching.
  • Head restraints: Adjust your head restraints to at least the level of the top of your head. This minimizes the risk of neck injuries, such as whiplash, in the event of a rear-end collision.
  • Mirrors: Adjust your rear-view and side mirrors to reduce blind spots. Side mirrors should be set so that only a small part of the side of your car is visible, maximizing the view of the road behind you. This helps you spot vehicles in adjacent lanes.

Seatbelts and Child Safety Restraints

The proper use of seatbelts and child restraints is critical for safety:

  • Seatbelt use: Wearing a seatbelt greatly reduces the risk of serious injury or death in a collision. The seatbelt should be snug across your chest and low over your hips. Never place the shoulder strap under your arm or behind your back, as this can cause severe injuries in a crash.

  • Child safety seats: The chapter explains the four stages of child safety restraints, based on the child’s age, weight, and height:

    1. Stage 1 — Rear-facing infant seat: For infants from birth until at least one year old and 9 kg (20 lbs). The seat should be installed in the back seat, positioned in the center if possible, and must never be placed in the front seat if there is an active airbag.
    2. Stage 2 — Forward-facing seat with tether: For children over one year old and weighing up to 18 kg (40 lbs). The seat should be used with a tether strap and remain in the back seat.
    3. Stage 3 — Booster seat: For children weighing over 18 kg (40 lbs) until they reach 145 cm (4’9”) or at least nine years old. A booster seat ensures the seatbelt fits properly across the child’s body, with the lap belt low over the hips and the shoulder belt across the chest.
    4. Stage 4 — Seatbelt only: Children can use a seatbelt without a booster seat once they outgrow it (at 145 cm or nine years old). It is recommended to keep children in the back seat until they are 12 years old for maximum safety.

Vehicle Controls

Understanding how to use your vehicle’s controls is essential for safe driving. The chapter provides an overview of key vehicle controls:

  • Steering wheel: Adjust the steering wheel so you can hold it comfortably with slightly bent arms. If the wheel is adjustable, make sure it’s in the right position before driving.
  • Ignition switch: Learn the positions of the ignition switch, which typically include lock (steering locked, ignition off), off (ignition off but steering not locked), accessory (for using electrical components like the radio), on (ignition on), and start (starting the engine).
  • Gearshift lever: Whether your vehicle has an automatic or manual transmission, familiarize yourself with the gearshift lever. In automatic cars, gears are selected automatically, but in manual cars, you need to shift gears depending on speed and road conditions.
  • Lights and wipers: Know how to use your headlights (low and high beams), hazard lights, and windshield wipers. This is especially important for driving in low visibility conditions like rain or fog.

Pre-trip and Periodic Checks

To ensure your vehicle is safe and road-ready, perform regular checks:

  • Pre-trip check: Before driving, check that your vehicle is safe to operate. This includes:

    • Ensuring all lights and turn signals are working.
    • Checking tire pressure and inspecting for damage.
    • Ensuring mirrors are adjusted properly and windows are clean.
    • Checking the fuel level and making sure there are no fluid leaks.

  • Periodic check: Every few weeks, do a more thorough check, including:

    • Checking engine oil, brake fluid, and power steering fluid levels.
    • Inspecting the tires for wear and ensuring they are properly inflated.
    • Ensuring the brakes are functioning well and inspecting hoses and belts for wear.

Prepare for Winter Driving

Winter conditions require extra precautions to ensure your vehicle performs safely:

  • Battery: Ensure your battery is in good condition, as cold weather can affect its performance.
  • Exhaust system: Check the exhaust system for leaks, as carbon monoxide can accumulate inside the car when windows are closed.
  • Fluids and tires: Replace engine oil and other fluids with winter-grade versions, and install four winter tires to improve traction on snow and ice. Be familiar with how to install tire chains if needed for extremely icy roads.

Driving and the Environment

This section encourages drivers to be mindful of their environmental impact:

  • Fuel efficiency: To reduce fuel consumption, drive smoothly, avoid sudden starts and stops, and maintain a steady speed. Planning your route and combining multiple errands into one trip also helps reduce fuel use.
  • Reducing emissions: Keep your vehicle well-maintained to reduce harmful emissions. This includes regular tune-ups, oil changes, and keeping your air filter clean. Driving habits like avoiding excessive idling, reducing the vehicle’s weight, and using the air conditioning sparingly also reduce your environmental footprint.

Overall, Chapter 2 highlights the importance of adjusting your vehicle for comfort and safety, performing regular maintenance checks, and making environmentally conscious decisions while driving.

Chapter 3

Chapter 3 of Learn to Drive Smart focuses on signs, signals, and road markings, which are essential for understanding how traffic flows and ensuring road safety. It provides a detailed overview of the most common signs and road markings, explaining how to interpret them by their shapes, colors, and symbols.

Signs

There are three key ways to identify road signs:

  1. Shape: The shape of the sign helps convey its message, such as the octagon shape for stop signs or diamond shapes for warnings.
  2. Color: Colors help categorize signs, such as red for prohibitions, yellow for warnings, and green for directions.
  3. Messages: Some signs include words or symbols to give additional instructions or information.

Regulatory Signs

Regulatory signs tell drivers about the laws they must follow. Ignoring these signs can lead to penalties or fines. Some key examples include:

  • Stop sign: Requires a complete stop before proceeding.
  • Yield sign: Instructs drivers to give the right of way to other vehicles or pedestrians before entering the intersection.
  • Speed limit signs: Indicate the maximum legal speed under ideal conditions. Sometimes signs also warn of lower speed limits ahead.
  • No entry signs: Indicate that vehicles are not allowed to enter that section of the road.
  • Do not pass signs: Restrict overtaking other vehicles in certain areas.

Some regulatory signs are also specific to seasonal conditions:

  • Winter tire or chain signs: Indicate that winter tires or chains are required when driving in snowy or icy conditions.

School, Playground, and Crosswalk Signs

These signs indicate areas where drivers need to be extra cautious due to the presence of children or pedestrians:

  • School zone signs: Typically display the speed limit (usually 30 km/h) during school hours or when children are present.
  • Playground zone signs: Indicate a reduced speed limit in areas where children may be playing, in effect from dawn to dusk every day.
  • Crosswalk signs: Indicate pedestrian crossings and may have flashing lights to alert drivers when pedestrians are crossing.

Lane Use Signs

Lane use signs dictate which lanes may be used for certain maneuvers or which vehicles are allowed to use specific lanes:

  • Turn control signs: Show which lanes are designated for turns or through traffic.
  • Reserved lane signs: Indicate lanes reserved for specific vehicles like buses or high-occupancy vehicles (HOVs).

Parking Signs

Parking signs let drivers know where they are allowed to park and the time limits for parking. Violating these signs may result in fines or your vehicle being towed:

  • Disabled parking: Indicates spots reserved for vehicles displaying the appropriate disability permit.
  • Time-limited parking: Restricts parking to certain hours or for a limited duration.

Warning Signs

Warning signs, usually diamond-shaped and yellow, provide alerts about upcoming hazards or changes in the road:

  • Winding road ahead: Alerts drivers to a series of curves.
  • Merging traffic: Warns drivers that vehicles will be entering the road from another lane.
  • Steep hill ahead: Alerts drivers to prepare for a steep descent, often suggesting a reduction in speed.
  • Slippery road ahead: Indicates areas where the road surface may be slippery due to rain, snow, or ice.

Signals

Traffic signals control the flow of vehicles at intersections and on roads. Drivers must follow these signals to ensure safe movement on the road.

Traffic Lights

  • Red light: Requires drivers to stop completely and wait until the light turns green.
  • Yellow light: Warns drivers that the light is about to turn red. Drivers should stop if safe to do so.
  • Green light: Allows drivers to proceed through the intersection, provided it is safe to do so.
  • Flashing red light: Functions like a stop sign, requiring drivers to stop and proceed when safe.
  • Flashing yellow light: Drivers must slow down and proceed with caution.

Lane Control Signals

Lane control signals, often found on bridges or tunnels, indicate whether lanes are open or closed to traffic:

  • Green arrow: Indicates that the lane is open for traffic.
  • Red “X”: Shows that the lane is closed and must not be used.

Road Markings

Road markings help guide traffic and indicate safe areas to drive, pass, or stop. Understanding these markings is crucial for safe driving.

Yellow Lines

  • Solid yellow line: Separates traffic moving in opposite directions. Crossing this line is not allowed.
  • Broken yellow line: Allows passing when it is safe to do so, but only when the broken line is on your side of the road.
  • Double solid yellow lines: Indicate that passing is not allowed in either direction.

White Lines

  • Solid white line: Separates lanes moving in the same direction. Crossing a solid white line is discouraged but not illegal.
  • Broken white line: Allows changing lanes when it is safe.

Reserved Lane Markings

Reserved lanes are marked by a white diamond symbol on the road, indicating that the lane is reserved for specific vehicles (e.g., buses, taxis, or HOVs).

Other Markings

  • Crosswalks: Indicate where pedestrians can cross the road safely. Drivers must yield to pedestrians in crosswalks.
  • Stop lines: Show where vehicles must stop at intersections or railway crossings.

Railway Crossing Signs

Railway crossing signs alert drivers to approaching train tracks. Some crossings have additional warning devices like lights or gates:

  • Railway crossing ahead: Warns drivers to be prepared to stop.
  • Railway crossing with stop sign: Requires a full stop before crossing, ensuring that no trains are coming.

Construction Signs

Construction zones require special attention due to potential hazards and reduced speed limits:

  • Construction ahead signs: Indicate that roadwork is being done ahead, and drivers should be cautious.
  • Traffic control person ahead: Alerts drivers that a person is directing traffic, and drivers must follow their instructions.

In construction zones, speeding fines are often doubled, and drivers must be especially careful around workers and equipment.

Information and Destination Signs

These signs provide drivers with helpful information about routes, destinations, and services available on the road:

  • Destination signs: Show distances to towns, cities, or highways.
  • Service signs: Indicate the location of nearby services like gas stations, restaurants, or hospitals.

Object Markers

Object markers are used to highlight objects near or on the road that could pose a hazard, such as guardrails or obstacles. These markers help drivers avoid collisions by guiding them to the safe side of the road or indicating a narrow passage.

Chapter 3 teaches drivers how to interpret the various signs, signals, and road markings they will encounter on the road. Understanding these traffic controls helps ensure safe driving by keeping vehicles moving in an organized and predictable manner.

Chapter 4

Chapter 4 of Learn to Drive Smart focuses on the rules of the road, helping drivers understand essential traffic laws and driving procedures. These rules ensure safety and proper flow of traffic, covering everything from intersections and lane usage to proper signaling and yielding practices.

Understanding Intersections

Intersections are where roads meet and are one of the most common areas for collisions. It’s essential to understand how to navigate them safely:

  • Controlled intersections: These are regulated by signs or signals, such as stop signs or traffic lights.
  • Uncontrolled intersections: Found in less populated areas, these intersections do not have signs or signals. Drivers must yield to the vehicle on their right.

At intersections, it is vital to:

  • Yield the right of way appropriately.
  • Observe and respect traffic signals and signs.
  • Be cautious of pedestrians and cyclists who may be crossing.

Signaling

Proper signaling is crucial for safe driving and for communicating your intentions to other road users:

  • Turn signals: Always signal well in advance (at least 30 meters) before turning or changing lanes. Use your signal even if no other vehicles are visible.
  • Brake lights: These automatically signal when you’re slowing down or stopping. Ensure they are functioning properly.
  • Hand signals: In case of light failure, you can use hand signals. For instance, extend your left arm straight out for a left turn, or point your arm upward for a right turn.

Types of Intersections

There are different types of intersections, each with its own rules:

  • Controlled intersections: These have traffic signals or signs, such as stop signs, yield signs, or traffic lights. Each driver must obey the indicated instruction.

    • Stop signs: At stop signs, drivers must come to a complete stop and proceed only when it is safe to do so.
    • Yield signs: Yield to other road users at the intersection. You may enter the intersection without stopping if the way is clear.

  • Uncontrolled intersections: At intersections without signs or signals, the basic rule is to yield to traffic coming from your right. If two vehicles arrive at the same time, the one on the right has the right of way.

  • Intersections with traffic lights: Follow the instructions of the lights. Green means go, yellow means prepare to stop, and red means stop. At some intersections, there are also special signals for left turns:

    • Advance green arrow: Allows protected left turns. When the arrow appears, vehicles turning left have the right of way.
    • Left-turn signals: At intersections with dedicated left-turn lights, vehicles can only turn left on the green arrow or signal.

Right-of-Way at Intersections

The rules for right-of-way ensure that traffic moves safely and predictably:

  • Four-way stops: At intersections where all roads have stop signs, the vehicle that arrives first goes first. If two vehicles arrive simultaneously, the one on the right goes first.
  • Turning left at an intersection: When turning left, you must yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians.
  • Traffic circles and roundabouts: These help traffic flow smoothly without requiring full stops. Drivers entering a traffic circle or roundabout must yield to traffic already in the circle and only enter when there is a safe gap.

Using Lanes Correctly

Correct lane usage is critical for smooth traffic flow and avoiding accidents:

  • Choosing the correct lane: On roads with multiple lanes, always stay in the right lane unless you are passing or preparing to turn left. On highways, slower traffic should stay to the right, and faster vehicles can pass on the left.
  • Lane tracking: Stay centered in your lane and avoid weaving. Use your mirrors and check blind spots before changing lanes.
  • Turning from correct lanes:
    • Right turns: Always turn from the rightmost lane into the rightmost lane.
    • Left turns: Turn from the leftmost lane and enter the corresponding left lane of the road you’re turning onto.

Turning Lanes and Special Lanes

  • Multiple turning lanes: When there are multiple lanes designated for turning, stay in your lane throughout the turn to avoid collisions.
  • Two-way left-turn lanes: In some areas, there are shared lanes in the center of the road marked with solid and dashed yellow lines. These are used by vehicles turning left from both directions. Enter the lane only when you’re ready to turn, and do not use it for passing.

U-turns

  • Legal U-turns: U-turns are allowed at many intersections unless a sign explicitly prohibits them. Always ensure that it is safe to complete the U-turn, and yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians.
  • Illegal U-turns: U-turns are prohibited in areas where visibility is limited, such as curves, hilltops, or places where signs specifically ban them.

Reserved Lanes

Reserved lanes are set aside for specific vehicles or purposes:

  • High Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes: Reserved for vehicles carrying two or more passengers (the number will be indicated on signs). These lanes help reduce traffic congestion by encouraging carpooling.
  • Bus lanes: These lanes are reserved for buses and sometimes for taxis or cyclists during certain hours. Regular vehicles are not allowed in these lanes except to make a turn.

Passing

Passing another vehicle is a common maneuver but must be done safely and legally:

  • Passing on the left: On most roads, pass only on the left. Ensure the lane is clear and signal your intention to pass. Do not exceed the speed limit while passing.
  • Passing on the right: Passing on the right is allowed on multi-lane roads when the vehicle in the left lane is turning left or if the lane is free. However, passing on the right is risky because the driver may not expect you to be there.

Merging

Merging requires cooperation between drivers:

  • Merging lanes: When merging onto a highway or when two lanes come together, adjust your speed to match the flow of traffic and enter the lane only when there is a sufficient gap. Vehicles already in the lane have the right of way.

Highway or Freeway Entrances and Exits

  • Entrance ramps: Use the entrance ramp to accelerate and match the speed of the vehicles on the highway. Signal early and merge smoothly.
  • Exit ramps: Signal well before the exit, and reduce your speed to match the posted limit on the ramp.

Parking Tips and Rules

Parking properly ensures safety and compliance with the law:

  • Parallel parking: Leave enough space between your vehicle and the curb. Ensure your vehicle does not block driveways or crosswalks.
  • Parking on hills: When parking on an incline, turn your wheels toward the curb if facing downhill and away from the curb if facing uphill. Always engage the parking brake.

Special Considerations

  • Cul-de-sacs: In residential areas, use caution when turning around in cul-de-sacs. Drive slowly and watch for pedestrians, children, or other hazards.
  • Turning around: If you need to turn your vehicle around mid-block, ensure it is legal to do so and that the area is clear of traffic and pedestrians.

Chapter 4 provides essential information on the rules and regulations that ensure safe driving. It covers proper procedures for intersections, lane usage, turning, and parking, giving drivers the knowledge they need to navigate roads safely and confidently.

Chapter 5

Chapter 5 of Learn to Drive Smart introduces the See-Think-Do strategy, which helps drivers anticipate and respond to potential hazards on the road. This approach emphasizes observation, assessment, and taking action to prevent collisions and maintain road safety.

See – Observation

Being a safe driver starts with observing your surroundings. Effective observation helps you spot potential hazards before they become a problem. The See step involves constantly scanning the road ahead, to the sides, and behind you.

  • Observing ahead: Always look far enough ahead to see potential hazards before reaching them. This gives you time to plan your next move. Check the road at least 12–15 seconds ahead.
  • Observing behind: Use your mirrors to regularly check what’s happening behind you. This helps you spot fast-approaching vehicles, changes in traffic flow, or potential hazards like tailgaters.
  • Observing at intersections: Before entering an intersection, look left, right, and then left again. Check for vehicles or pedestrians that might be entering your path.

Other important elements of observation:

  • Blind spots: These are areas around your vehicle that you cannot see using your mirrors. Always shoulder check before changing lanes or merging to ensure no vehicles are in your blind spots.
  • Hazard perception: Spotting hazards early helps you avoid potential collisions. Hazards can include other vehicles, pedestrians, cyclists, animals, and changing road conditions (e.g., icy roads or construction zones).

Think – Assessing the Risk

Once you’ve observed a potential hazard, the next step is to Think—assess the risk and decide what action to take. This step involves using judgment to decide how to respond to what you’ve seen.

  • Assess the risk: Consider how likely the hazard is to affect your path and how serious the potential danger is. For example, if you see a pedestrian near the road, assess whether they are likely to step into your lane.
  • Predict what will happen: Based on your observations, predict how the situation may unfold. For instance, if you notice a vehicle approaching a stop sign quickly, predict whether the driver might not stop in time.
  • Consider your options: Think about the safest course of action. Can you slow down or stop? Should you change lanes? Planning ahead and considering different responses gives you time to make the best decision.

Do – Taking Action

After observing and assessing the situation, the final step is to Do—take the appropriate action to avoid or respond to the hazard. This involves controlling your vehicle in a way that keeps you and others safe.

  • Speed control: Adjust your speed as necessary. Slowing down or stopping can help you avoid collisions. Always be prepared to reduce your speed when approaching intersections, curves, or areas with pedestrians.
  • Steering: Keep control of your steering. If you need to change lanes or avoid an obstacle, do so smoothly and safely. Quick, jerky movements can cause loss of control.
  • Braking: Use your brakes correctly based on the situation. Gradually applying the brakes is often the best course, but in an emergency, you may need to brake more forcefully to avoid a collision.

Applying the See-Think-Do Strategy

The chapter highlights how the See-Think-Do strategy can help in a variety of driving situations, such as:

  • Approaching intersections: By observing early, you can predict whether other vehicles or pedestrians might enter your path, allowing you to slow down or stop in time.
  • Handling curves: Look ahead to anticipate any sharp curves or changes in the road. Slow down before you enter the curve, and maintain a safe speed throughout.
  • Dealing with pedestrians and cyclists: Always be alert to the presence of pedestrians or cyclists, especially in residential areas or near schools. Be prepared to yield and give them space on the road.
  • Responding to space conflicts: When other vehicles are too close or crowd your lane, anticipate the need to adjust your position or speed to avoid a collision.

Space Margins

Maintaining a safe distance from other vehicles is essential to avoiding collisions. The chapter emphasizes using space margins to protect yourself and others on the road:

  • Space in front: The two-second rule helps you maintain a safe distance from the vehicle in front. Choose a stationary object on the road and start counting when the car ahead passes it. If it takes less than two seconds for you to reach the same object, you’re following too closely. In poor weather conditions, extend this to three or four seconds.
  • Space behind: Watch for vehicles following too closely. If someone is tailgating you, slow down slightly to encourage them to pass or move into another lane if possible.
  • Space beside: Ensure there’s enough space on either side of your vehicle to maneuver if necessary. This can be especially important when driving next to large vehicles like trucks.

Choosing a Safe Gap

When merging, turning, or changing lanes, always ensure there is a sufficient gap between your vehicle and others:

  • Merging: Adjust your speed to match the flow of traffic and merge when there is a clear gap.
  • Turning at intersections: When making a turn, especially left turns, ensure there’s enough time and space for you to complete the turn safely without cutting off oncoming traffic.

Communicating with Other Drivers

Communication helps prevent misunderstandings and keeps traffic flowing smoothly:

  • Turn signals: Use your signals to indicate your intentions to other drivers. Signal well in advance of making turns or changing lanes.
  • Brake lights: Tap your brakes lightly to alert drivers behind you that you are slowing down.
  • Horn: The horn is a tool for alerting other road users of potential hazards. Use it sparingly and only when necessary to prevent a collision.
  • Eye contact: When interacting with pedestrians, cyclists, or other drivers, making eye contact can help confirm their intentions and prevent accidents.

Vision Blocks and Poor Road Conditions

The chapter discusses how to handle situations with limited visibility, such as when your view is blocked by other vehicles or objects:

  • Vision blocks: If your view is blocked by a larger vehicle, slow down and increase your following distance. Move slightly to the left or right within your lane to improve your sightline.
  • Poor road conditions: In rain, snow, or fog, reduce your speed and increase following distance. Use low-beam headlights in foggy conditions, as high beams can reflect back and reduce visibility.

Surprises and Hazards

Unexpected situations can arise at any time. The chapter encourages drivers to always be prepared for surprises such as:

  • Sudden stops: Vehicles ahead may stop abruptly. Maintain a safe following distance and be ready to stop if necessary.
  • Animals on the road: Slow down in areas where animals are common. If an animal crosses your path, brake firmly but avoid swerving, as this can cause a more severe accident.

Chapter 5 outlines the See-Think-Do strategy as a crucial approach to safe driving. By focusing on observation, assessment, and action, drivers can anticipate hazards, make informed decisions, and respond effectively to prevent accidents.

Chapter 6

Chapter 6 of Learn to Drive Smart covers basic driving skills and provides practical guidance on how to handle different driving situations and environments safely. This chapter emphasizes developing strong driving habits and techniques to navigate common road scenarios.

Starting, Stopping, and Steering

Mastering basic vehicle control is essential for safe driving. This section explains the fundamental skills required to start, stop, and steer your vehicle.

  • Starting the vehicle: Before starting, make sure your seatbelt is fastened, your mirrors are adjusted, and your foot is on the brake. Start the engine smoothly without pressing the accelerator unless necessary.
  • Smooth starts: Gently press the accelerator to move forward smoothly. Avoid jerking movements, especially in stop-and-go traffic.
  • Stopping: Apply the brakes gradually to come to a smooth stop. Always maintain control and avoid sudden stops unless it’s an emergency.
  • Steering: Keep both hands on the steering wheel in the “9 and 3” or “8 and 4” positions for better control. Use a smooth and steady steering motion to maintain lane position and make turns.

Driving Forward

This section focuses on maintaining control while driving straight ahead on various road types.

  • Lane position: Stay centered in your lane. Keep a steady speed and avoid weaving. Adjust your position if the road narrows or if obstacles are present, such as parked cars or cyclists.
  • Speed control: Adjust your speed based on the road conditions, traffic flow, and the posted speed limit. It’s important to drive at a speed that allows you to react to any changes in traffic or road conditions.
  • Keeping a safe distance: Maintain a safe following distance from the vehicle ahead using the two-second rule. Increase this distance in poor weather conditions or when driving at higher speeds.

Reversing

Reversing requires special attention since your view is more limited. This section provides tips for safely backing up.

  • Reverse slowly: Always back up slowly and cautiously, checking your surroundings and mirrors frequently. Use your rearview camera if available, but don’t rely on it exclusively.
  • Check surroundings: Before reversing, ensure there are no pedestrians, cyclists, or obstacles behind your vehicle. Always check blind spots by looking over your shoulder.
  • Steering in reverse: When backing up, turn the steering wheel in the direction you want the rear of your vehicle to go. Use small steering adjustments to avoid oversteering.

Changing Lanes

Changing lanes is a frequent driving maneuver that requires proper signaling, observation, and timing.

  • Signal early: Signal your intention to change lanes well in advance. This lets other drivers know what you plan to do.
  • Check mirrors and blind spots: Always check your mirrors and blind spots before changing lanes to ensure it’s safe. Use shoulder checks to confirm no vehicles are in your blind spots.
  • Move smoothly: When it’s safe, move gradually into the new lane without making abrupt steering movements. Avoid changing lanes in intersections or heavy traffic unless necessary.

Turning

Safe turning requires proper timing, signaling, and speed control. This section covers right and left turns as well as U-turns.

  • Right turns: Signal at least 30 meters before the turn. Slow down as you approach the turn and check for pedestrians and cyclists. Stay close to the curb as you make the turn, and enter the right lane of the new road.
  • Left turns: Signal well before reaching the intersection. Yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians. Make the turn only when it’s safe, and enter the left lane of the new road.
  • U-turns: U-turns are legal only where indicated by signage and where it’s safe. Always ensure there’s enough space and time to complete the U-turn without interfering with other traffic.

Parking

Parking safely and legally is an important skill, especially in busy urban areas. The chapter covers different types of parking:

  • Parallel parking: Signal and position your vehicle about one meter from the car you’ll be parking behind. Reverse slowly, turning your wheel toward the curb to guide your vehicle into the space. Straighten out once your car is in the spot.
  • Angle parking: Angle parking is common in parking lots and requires you to enter the space at an angle. Signal your intention and align your vehicle with the space. Slowly move into the spot, adjusting as needed to stay centered.
  • Hill parking: When parking on a hill, turn your wheels toward the curb if you’re facing downhill and away from the curb if you’re facing uphill. Always engage your parking brake to prevent the vehicle from rolling.

Handling Intersections

Navigating intersections is one of the most complex driving tasks due to the number of variables involved, such as other drivers, pedestrians, and cyclists.

  • Controlled intersections: Follow traffic lights, signs, and signals to determine right of way. Stop at stop signs and red lights, and proceed when it’s safe.
  • Uncontrolled intersections: At intersections without signs or signals, yield to the vehicle on your right. Slow down and be prepared to stop if necessary.
  • Pedestrian safety: Always yield to pedestrians at crosswalks and intersections. Be alert for pedestrians crossing from both sides, and ensure you make eye contact with them when necessary.

Dealing with Pedestrians and Cyclists

  • Yield to pedestrians: Pedestrians always have the right of way at crosswalks and intersections. Slow down in areas where pedestrians are common, such as schools and residential neighborhoods.
  • Share the road with cyclists: Cyclists have the right to share the road. Give cyclists at least one meter of space when passing, and be cautious when making turns or opening doors next to bike lanes.

Railway Crossings

Railway crossings present a unique hazard for drivers. Follow these guidelines for safe crossing:

  • Slow down: As you approach a railway crossing, slow down and prepare to stop if necessary.
  • Check for trains: Look both ways, even if there are no lights or gates. Only cross the tracks when you’re sure there are no trains coming.
  • Stop if needed: If a train is approaching, stop at least five meters from the nearest rail. Do not stop on the tracks or attempt to cross if the train is too close.

Handling Curves

  • Slow down before entering: Always slow down before entering a curve. Going too fast around a curve can cause you to lose control.
  • Maintain steady speed: Once in the curve, maintain a steady speed to keep control of your vehicle. Avoid braking sharply, as this can lead to skidding.

Freeway Driving

Driving on freeways requires special skills due to higher speeds and heavy traffic:

  • Merging: Use the acceleration lane to match the speed of the freeway traffic and merge smoothly into the flow. Always check your blind spots before merging.
  • Lane discipline: Stay in the right lane unless passing. Use the left lane only to pass or if you need to exit.
  • Exiting: Plan your exit ahead of time and signal early. Move to the exit lane in advance and reduce your speed as you enter the exit ramp.

Chapter 6 provides practical advice for mastering basic driving skills and handling different situations. It emphasizes the importance of smooth, controlled movements, proper signaling, and staying alert to ensure safety on the road.

Chapter 7

Chapter 7 of Learn to Drive Smart covers emergency situations and challenging driving conditions, offering strategies to help drivers stay safe and respond effectively to unexpected hazards or difficult driving environments. This chapter focuses on how to handle various emergencies and how to drive safely in conditions like rain, snow, fog, and darkness.

Handling Emergency Situations

Emergencies on the road can arise quickly, and knowing how to respond is critical to avoiding accidents or reducing the severity of a situation.

Brake Failure

  • Response: If your brakes fail, shift into a lower gear to help slow down the vehicle. Pump the brake pedal quickly to try to build up brake pressure. If that fails, use the emergency brake gradually to stop the vehicle.
  • Preventive Tip: Regularly check your brakes and brake fluid levels to reduce the chances of brake failure.

Tire Blowout

  • Response: Keep a firm grip on the steering wheel and steer straight. Don’t slam on the brakes; instead, ease off the accelerator and allow the vehicle to slow down gradually. Pull over to the side of the road when it is safe to do so.
  • Preventive Tip: Regularly inspect your tires for wear and proper inflation. Replace worn-out tires before they fail.

Engine Failure

  • Response: If your engine stalls, signal and steer the vehicle to the side of the road. Shift into neutral or park and try restarting the engine. If it doesn’t restart, call for roadside assistance.
  • Preventive Tip: Keep your vehicle well-maintained by checking oil levels, coolant, and other vital engine components regularly.

Steering Failure

  • Response: If your steering fails, slow down gradually by easing off the accelerator. Turn on your hazard lights and try to guide the vehicle safely to the side of the road. If your steering locks while turning, reduce speed as much as possible before stopping.
  • Preventive Tip: Regular vehicle maintenance can help prevent steering issues. If your vehicle uses power steering, ensure the fluid is at the correct level.

Stuck Accelerator

  • Response: If your accelerator sticks, shift to neutral and apply the brakes to slow down. Do not turn off the ignition, as this could lock the steering. Guide your vehicle to a safe location and stop. Once stopped, turn off the engine.
  • Preventive Tip: Keep the footwell area clear to prevent objects from interfering with the pedals. Have the accelerator checked if it feels sticky or unresponsive.

Skidding

  • Response: If your vehicle begins to skid, ease off the accelerator and steer gently in the direction you want the front of the car to go. Avoid using the brakes as this can worsen the skid.
  • Preventive Tip: Drive at a safe speed, especially on wet or icy roads. Ensure your tires are in good condition with adequate tread depth.

Driving in Challenging Conditions

Driving in adverse weather or low visibility conditions requires extra caution and adjusted driving techniques to stay safe.

Rain

  • Visibility and traction: Rain reduces visibility and makes roads slippery, increasing the risk of hydroplaning.
  • Response: Slow down, turn on your headlights, and increase the distance between you and the vehicle ahead. Avoid sudden stops and turns to reduce the chance of skidding or hydroplaning.
  • Hydroplaning: If your vehicle starts to hydroplane (lose traction on a wet road), ease off the accelerator and steer straight. Don’t brake suddenly.

Snow and Ice

  • Traction and control: Snow and ice significantly reduce traction, making it harder to stop and control the vehicle.
  • Response: Slow down and maintain a longer following distance. Avoid sudden braking or sharp turns, and gently accelerate and decelerate. Use snow tires and consider carrying tire chains in case of extreme conditions.
  • Skidding on ice: If you begin to skid on ice, steer gently in the direction of the skid and avoid braking.

Fog

  • Reduced visibility: Fog can greatly reduce your ability to see other vehicles, pedestrians, and road markings.
  • Response: Use low-beam headlights, as high beams can reflect off the fog and further reduce visibility. Drive slowly and increase the following distance between your vehicle and others. Use roadside reflectors as a guide, and consider pulling over if visibility becomes too poor to drive safely.

Driving in the Dark

  • Visibility issues: Driving at night reduces your ability to see hazards ahead. Depth perception, peripheral vision, and color recognition are also reduced.
  • Response: Use your headlights, and switch to low beams when following other vehicles or when oncoming traffic is approaching. Slow down and keep your windshield clean to reduce glare.
  • Fatigue: Driving at night can increase the chances of driver fatigue. If you feel drowsy, pull over to rest or switch drivers if possible.

Sun Glare

  • Visibility challenges: Driving into the sun can make it difficult to see the road ahead or vehicles around you.
  • Response: Use sunglasses and your sun visor to reduce glare. Increase your following distance and be prepared for sudden stops by other drivers.

Wind

  • Strong gusts: High winds can affect your ability to steer and control your vehicle, especially if driving a larger or lighter vehicle.
  • Response: Slow down and keep both hands on the steering wheel. Be cautious when passing large trucks, as they can create strong wind drafts.

Responding to Collisions

In the event of a collision, it’s important to know what to do to protect yourself and others.

At the Scene of a Collision

  • Stop immediately: It’s illegal to leave the scene of a collision. Pull over safely and stop your vehicle.
  • Check for injuries: Check yourself, your passengers, and others involved for injuries. If anyone is hurt, call 911 immediately.
  • Exchange information: Get the names, addresses, and insurance details of everyone involved in the collision, including witnesses if possible.
  • Document the scene: Take photos of the scene, including any damage to vehicles, road conditions, and any relevant traffic signs.
  • Report the collision: If the damage exceeds a certain amount (typically specified by local laws) or if there are injuries, you must report the collision to the police.

If You Are Involved in a Minor Collision

  • Move vehicles: If there are no injuries and the vehicles are drivable, move them to the side of the road to avoid blocking traffic.
  • Exchange information: Even in minor collisions, you need to exchange information with the other driver.

If You Hit a Parked Vehicle

  • Leave a note: If the owner isn’t present, leave a note with your name, phone number, and insurance details on the parked vehicle.

Vehicle Breakdowns

Knowing how to handle a breakdown can help keep you and others safe while you wait for help.

  • Pull over safely: Move your vehicle off the road as far as possible. If you’re on a highway, aim for the shoulder.
  • Turn on hazard lights: Use your hazard lights to alert other drivers to your breakdown.
  • Stay in the vehicle: If it’s safe to do so, stay inside your vehicle while you wait for assistance, especially if you’re on a busy road. Call for roadside assistance.
  • Emergency kit: Keep an emergency kit in your vehicle, which includes items like a flashlight, blankets, water, and a first-aid kit.

Chapter 7 provides essential tips on how to handle emergency situations and difficult driving conditions, such as brake failure, tire blowouts, and adverse weather. The chapter emphasizes the importance of staying calm, reacting quickly, and taking preventive measures to minimize risks.

Chapter 8

Chapter 8 of Learn to Drive Smart covers personal strategies for safe driving, emphasizing the importance of being proactive about maintaining your driving skills and staying fit to drive. It focuses on managing personal factors such as fatigue, stress, and impairment that can affect your driving ability, as well as how to develop safe driving habits for life.

Staying Fit to Drive

Being fit to drive means more than just avoiding impairment by alcohol or drugs. Your physical, mental, and emotional state all play a role in how well you drive.

Managing Fatigue

Fatigue is one of the leading causes of crashes, especially on long trips. Even if you don’t fall asleep, fatigue slows your reaction time and impairs your judgment.

  • Causes of fatigue: Lack of sleep, driving long distances without breaks, stress, and certain medications can make you drowsy.
  • Preventing fatigue:
    • Get a good night’s sleep before a long trip.
    • Take breaks every two hours or 160 kilometers.
    • Share the driving with a passenger, if possible.
    • Avoid driving during times when you would normally be sleeping.
  • Signs of fatigue: Frequent yawning, drifting out of your lane, difficulty focusing, and feeling drowsy are signs you should pull over and rest.

Dealing with Stress and Emotions

Emotional stress, anger, or frustration can cloud your judgment and cause aggressive driving behavior.

  • Stay calm: If you’re upset, calm down before getting behind the wheel. Don’t drive while angry, as it may lead to road rage or poor decision-making.
  • Managing road rage: If another driver behaves aggressively, avoid making eye contact or reacting. If the situation escalates, find a safe place to pull over and let the aggressive driver pass.

Impairment by Drugs or Alcohol

Driving under the influence of drugs or alcohol is extremely dangerous and is one of the leading causes of crashes.

  • Effects of alcohol: Alcohol impairs your ability to focus, slows your reaction time, and affects your decision-making skills. Even small amounts can impact your driving ability.
  • Drugs and driving: Both illegal and prescription drugs can impair your ability to drive safely. This includes recreational drugs, some pain medications, and drugs that cause drowsiness or slow your reaction times.
  • Alternatives to driving impaired: If you have been drinking or are impaired by drugs, don’t drive. Use public transit, call a taxi, or arrange for a designated driver.
  • Plan ahead: If you know you’ll be drinking, plan your transportation in advance.

Developing Safe Driving Habits

Safe driving habits are essential for reducing the risk of crashes and staying safe on the road. This section emphasizes the importance of adopting lifelong safe driving behaviors.

Attitude and Awareness

Driving is a complex task that requires focus, patience, and awareness of your surroundings.

  • Positive attitude: Stay focused on safety, respect other road users, and avoid aggressive driving behaviors like tailgating or speeding.
  • Be aware of your surroundings: Continuously scan the road ahead, behind, and to the sides to anticipate potential hazards.

Driving Defensively

Defensive driving involves being prepared for the actions of other road users, anticipating potential hazards, and being ready to take corrective action if necessary.

  • Anticipate other drivers: Always assume other drivers may make mistakes. For example, be prepared for sudden lane changes, cars running red lights, or pedestrians unexpectedly stepping into the road.
  • Maintain a safe following distance: Use the two-second rule to keep a safe distance from the vehicle in front. Increase this distance in bad weather or heavy traffic.
  • Avoid distractions: Focus on driving and avoid using your phone, eating, or any other activity that takes your attention away from the road.
  • Be prepared for emergencies: Know how to respond to common road emergencies, such as skids, tire blowouts, and brake failures.

Distractions

Distracted driving is a major cause of collisions. Anything that takes your attention away from the task of driving is a distraction.

  • Common distractions:
    • Using a cellphone (texting, calling, or using apps).
    • Adjusting the radio or GPS.
    • Talking to passengers.
    • Eating or drinking.
  • Minimize distractions:
    • Set up your GPS, playlist, or climate control before starting to drive.
    • If you must use your phone, pull over in a safe location.
    • Keep your focus on the road and avoid engaging in tasks that can take your attention away from driving.

Impairment from Prescription and Over-the-Counter Drugs

Even legal medications can impair your driving ability.

  • Prescription drugs: Some medications for pain, anxiety, or sleep can slow your reaction time or make you drowsy. Always check with your doctor or pharmacist about whether it’s safe to drive while taking certain medications.
  • Over-the-counter drugs: Cold and allergy medications, as well as sleep aids, can also impair your ability to drive. Always read the warning labels on over-the-counter medications.

Seniors and Driving

As you age, your ability to drive safely may change due to physical and mental health changes.

  • Common challenges:
    • Reduced vision or hearing.
    • Slower reaction times.
    • Difficulty with multitasking.
  • Maintaining driving fitness: Seniors should get regular eye exams and consult their doctor about any medications that may affect their driving. Staying physically active can help maintain the strength and flexibility needed for driving.
  • Know when to stop driving: It’s important to recognize when it’s time to reduce or stop driving altogether. If driving becomes stressful or difficult, or if there are frequent close calls or confusion while driving, it may be time to evaluate your ability to drive safely.

Young Drivers

Young drivers, especially those in their teens, are more likely to be involved in crashes due to inexperience and risk-taking behaviors.

  • Developing good habits: It’s important for young drivers to focus on building safe driving habits from the start. Avoid distractions, practice defensive driving, and never drive under the influence of drugs or alcohol.
  • Graduated Licensing Program (GLP): Many jurisdictions have a graduated licensing program for new drivers that includes learner and novice phases, with certain restrictions to help young drivers gain experience safely.

Health and Driving

Physical health can affect your ability to drive safely. Certain conditions and medications can impair vision, reaction time, or motor skills.

  • Regular health checkups: Ensure that your vision, hearing, and overall physical health are sufficient for safe driving. Conditions like diabetes, sleep apnea, or epilepsy may need to be monitored to ensure they don’t affect your driving ability.
  • Adaptations for health conditions: In some cases, drivers may need special adaptations or equipment to help them drive safely, such as hand controls for drivers with mobility issues.

Driver Refresher Courses

Driver refresher courses are a great way to stay up to date on the latest road rules and to improve your driving skills.

  • Benefits of refresher courses:
    • Helps keep you informed about new laws or changes to traffic regulations.
    • Provides opportunities to improve defensive driving techniques.
    • Can reduce insurance premiums in some cases.

Chapter 8 emphasizes the importance of maintaining your fitness to drive and adopting safe driving habits. It encourages drivers to manage personal factors like fatigue, stress, and impairment and highlights how developing a positive attitude and driving defensively can lead to a lifetime of safe driving.

Chapter 9

Chapter 9 of Learn to Drive Smart focuses on getting your driver’s licence, outlining the steps and requirements for obtaining and maintaining a driver’s licence in British Columbia. This chapter explains the Graduated Licensing Program (GLP) and provides information about the tests and licensing process.

Graduated Licensing Program (GLP)

The Graduated Licensing Program (GLP) is designed to help new drivers gain experience gradually and safely. It involves three stages: learner, novice, and full licence. Each stage has its own set of rules and restrictions aimed at improving driving skills over time.

Learner Stage (L)

The learner stage is the first step toward getting a driver’s licence. During this stage, new drivers must practice driving under supervision and follow specific rules.

  • Eligibility: You must be at least 16 years old to apply for a learner’s licence.
  • Knowledge test: You’ll need to pass a knowledge test to get your learner’s licence. This test covers road signs, rules of the road, and safe driving practices.
  • Learner’s licence conditions:
    • Supervisor: You must be accompanied by a licensed supervisor who is 25 years or older and has held a full licence for at least two years.
    • Passenger limit: You are allowed only one passenger in addition to your supervisor, unless they are immediate family members.
    • No alcohol: You must maintain a zero blood alcohol content (BAC) while driving.
    • L sign: You must display an “L” sign on the back of your vehicle while driving.
    • Driving hours: You are not permitted to drive between midnight and 5 a.m.
    • Duration: You must hold your learner’s licence for at least 12 months before advancing to the next stage (8 months if you complete an approved driver education program).

Novice Stage (N)

The novice stage is the second phase of the GLP, where drivers can drive on their own but must still follow certain restrictions to ensure safety.

  • Eligibility: After holding your learner’s licence for at least 12 months and passing the Class 7 road test, you can move to the novice stage.
  • Novice licence conditions:
    • Passenger limit: You are allowed only one passenger unless accompanied by a qualified supervisor or unless the passengers are immediate family members.
    • Zero alcohol: You must maintain a zero BAC while driving.
    • N sign: You must display an “N” sign on the back of your vehicle while driving.
    • Duration: You must hold your novice licence for at least 24 months before applying for a full licence. This period can be reduced to 18 months if you complete an approved driver education program.

Full Licence (Class 5)

After completing the novice stage, drivers are eligible to apply for a full licence.

  • Eligibility: After holding your novice licence for 24 months (or 18 months with driver education), you can take the Class 5 road test.
  • Class 5 road test: This test assesses your ability to drive safely and confidently in a variety of conditions. If you pass the test, you will receive your full driver’s licence.

Road Tests

There are two road tests as part of the GLP: the Class 7 road test and the Class 5 road test.

Class 7 Road Test

The Class 7 road test is taken after the learner stage and is required to move to the novice stage.

  • Test content: The test evaluates your basic driving skills, including stopping, turning, lane changes, and obeying traffic signs and signals.
  • Preparation: Practice driving in various conditions and review the driving techniques covered in this guide. Take a driver education course if possible.

Class 5 Road Test

The Class 5 road test is taken after the novice stage and is required to obtain a full licence.

  • Test content: This test evaluates your advanced driving skills, including highway driving, merging, and handling complex traffic situations.
  • Preparation: Continue to practice driving in a variety of situations, including night driving and driving in bad weather, to ensure you are ready for the test.

Knowledge Test

To obtain a learner’s licence, you must pass a written knowledge test. The knowledge test covers:

  • Road signs: Identification and meaning of various road signs.
  • Rules of the road: Understanding road rules, speed limits, right-of-way, and other traffic laws.
  • Safe driving practices: Questions about defensive driving, emergency handling, and how to drive in different weather conditions.

Vision and Medical Requirements

  • Vision test: You will need to pass a vision test to ensure you can see well enough to drive. If you require glasses or contact lenses, you must wear them when driving.
  • Medical conditions: Some medical conditions may affect your ability to drive safely. You may be required to submit a medical report if you have a condition that could impair your driving ability. This report helps determine whether you need driving restrictions or adaptations.

Driver Training and Education

Taking a driver training course can help you become a safer, more skilled driver. It can also reduce the time you need to spend in the learner or novice stage.

  • Driver education programs: These programs provide professional instruction on driving techniques, defensive driving, and road safety.
  • Benefits of driver training:
    • Reduces your time in the GLP.
    • Prepares you for the road tests and real-world driving conditions.
    • Helps you develop good driving habits from the start.

Maintaining Your Licence

Once you have your licence, it’s important to follow the rules and drive safely to maintain it.

  • Licence renewals: You will need to renew your licence periodically, depending on your age and the type of licence you hold.
  • Suspensions and penalties: Traffic violations, such as speeding, impaired driving, or failing to wear a seatbelt, can lead to demerit points and licence suspensions. Accumulating too many points can result in your licence being suspended.

Demerit Points

Demerit points are added to your driving record for traffic violations. Accumulating too many demerit points can lead to penalties, such as fines, licence suspensions, or mandatory driver improvement courses.

  • Points for violations: Common violations, such as speeding or failing to yield, can result in demerit points. Serious offenses, such as impaired driving, result in more points.
  • Consequences: Accumulating 12 or more points in a two-year period can lead to a licence suspension.

Getting an Enhanced Driver’s Licence (EDL)

An Enhanced Driver’s Licence (EDL) allows you to use your licence for crossing the U.S. border by land or sea without a passport.

  • Eligibility: Canadian citizens who hold a valid B.C. driver’s licence are eligible to apply for an EDL.
  • Application process: You will need to provide proof of Canadian citizenship, residency, and identity to obtain an EDL.

It outlines the requirements for each stage of the licensing process, as well as the importance of driver education and safe driving practices to ensure long-term success as a licensed driver.

Chapter 10

Chapter 10 of Learn to Drive Smart is the final chapter, focusing on your responsibilities as a driver and the broader impacts of your driving on society, the environment, and other road users. It highlights the importance of adopting a responsible attitude toward driving and being aware of how your actions can influence the safety and well-being of others.

Driving and the Law

When you drive, you are responsible for following the rules of the road and complying with the law. Breaking these laws can have serious consequences, including fines, penalties, licence suspensions, and, in severe cases, imprisonment.

Understanding Traffic Laws

  • Traffic laws: These are designed to regulate traffic flow and keep everyone on the road safe. They include rules about speed limits, right-of-way, and the use of seatbelts.
  • Penalties for violations: Violating traffic laws, such as speeding or impaired driving, can result in fines, demerit points, and even the loss of your licence. The chapter stresses the importance of being aware of the consequences of breaking the law and taking your driving responsibilities seriously.

Impaired Driving

  • Severe penalties: Driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs can result in severe penalties, including criminal charges, licence suspensions, and even imprisonment. The risks of impaired driving include serious injury or death, not only to the driver but to other road users as well.
  • Zero alcohol: As part of the Graduated Licensing Program (GLP), new drivers are required to have zero blood alcohol content (BAC) while driving. This rule helps prevent new drivers from taking risks that could endanger themselves and others.

Driving and the Environment

Your driving habits affect not only road safety but also the environment. Adopting environmentally friendly driving practices can help reduce pollution, save fuel, and decrease your environmental footprint.

Reducing Your Environmental Impact

  • Eco-friendly driving: The chapter highlights the importance of driving efficiently by reducing excessive speeding, avoiding rapid acceleration, and using smooth braking. Driving at a steady speed reduces fuel consumption and lowers emissions.
  • Vehicle maintenance: Keeping your vehicle properly maintained can also help reduce environmental impact. Regular tune-ups, proper tire inflation, and oil changes help your car run more efficiently and emit fewer pollutants.
  • Carpooling and public transportation: Reducing the number of trips you take by car, carpooling with others, or using public transit are other ways to lower your environmental footprint.

Fuel Efficiency Tips

  • Avoid idling: Idling wastes fuel and increases emissions. Turn off your engine if you’re going to be stopped for more than a minute.
  • Plan your trips: Combine errands into a single trip to reduce the amount of time spent driving.
  • Use the right gear: In manual vehicles, use the appropriate gear to avoid overworking the engine and consuming more fuel.

Sharing the Road

Being aware of and respectful toward other road users is a key part of being a responsible driver. This includes not only other drivers but also pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists.

Respecting Other Road Users

  • Pedestrians: Always yield to pedestrians at crosswalks and intersections. Be especially cautious in areas where there are children, such as school zones or parks.
  • Cyclists: Cyclists have the right to share the road. Give them plenty of space when passing and be mindful of bike lanes.
  • Motorcyclists: Motorcycles are smaller and more vulnerable than cars. Be cautious when driving near motorcycles, as they may be harder to see in your blind spots.
  • Large vehicles: Give extra space to large vehicles like trucks and buses, as they have larger blind spots and take longer to stop.

Social Responsibility

Driving is a privilege that comes with social responsibility. Safe driving not only protects you but also ensures the safety of others on the road. The chapter emphasizes that your actions as a driver can have significant consequences for others, and you should always drive with care and consideration for those around you.

Being a Role Model

  • For young drivers: Experienced drivers are role models for young or new drivers. Practicing safe, responsible driving sets a good example for others and encourages the development of good driving habits.
  • For passengers: As a driver, you are responsible for your passengers’ safety. This includes ensuring that everyone wears seatbelts and follows the rules of the road.

Vehicle Maintenance and Safety

Maintaining your vehicle is essential for keeping it safe and roadworthy. A poorly maintained vehicle can pose a danger to you and others.

Regular Maintenance

  • Safety checks: Perform regular safety checks on your vehicle, including inspecting tires, brakes, lights, and fluid levels. Ensure that your windshield wipers are in good condition and that your windows are clean for optimal visibility.
  • Vehicle recalls: Keep up to date with any vehicle recalls that may affect the safety or performance of your car. If your vehicle is recalled, take it to the dealership for the necessary repairs.

End-of-Life Vehicle Disposal

When your vehicle reaches the end of its useful life, it’s important to dispose of it responsibly.

  • Recycling programs: Many parts of a vehicle can be recycled or reused. Participate in vehicle recycling programs to ensure that your car is disposed of in an environmentally friendly way.

Insurance and Financial Responsibility

Having proper insurance is a legal requirement and an essential part of driving responsibly. Insurance protects you financially in case of an accident and helps cover the costs of damage or injury.

Types of Insurance

  • Basic insurance: This covers third-party liability, which pays for damage or injuries you cause to others in a collision. In British Columbia, basic insurance is mandatory.
  • Optional insurance: Additional coverage can include collision insurance (which covers damage to your own vehicle), comprehensive insurance (which covers theft, fire, and vandalism), and underinsured motorist protection.

Insurance Costs

  • Factors affecting cost: Your driving record, the type of vehicle you drive, and your age and experience can all affect your insurance premiums. Safe driving habits can help keep your insurance costs lower.

Chapter 10 emphasizes the broader responsibilities that come with driving, such as obeying traffic laws, reducing environmental impact, sharing the road, and maintaining proper insurance. It also stresses the importance of vehicle maintenance and social responsibility, reminding drivers that their actions have an impact on others both on and off the road.
